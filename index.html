Course Description

The aim of this course is to study the latest research and development in analyzing the security and privacy of emerging networks and technologies. We will study different traditional and AI-based security analysis techniques. For traditional techniques, we will study fuzzing, formal verification, model checking, and static and dynamic analysis techniques. For AI-based techniques, we will focus on different agentic AI techniques from both academic research and industry (see Aardvark from OpenAI: https://openai.com/index/introducing-aardvark/ ). The course aims to lay the foundation for complex network and system analysis, such as 5G and NextG networks, Satellite Communication, and emerging IoT protocols like Matter.

Course Objectives

  • Ability to understand the details of vulnerabilities in networks and systems.
  • Ability to understand the foundations of applying formal verification for complex systems.
  • Ability to understand and apply static and dynamic security testing tools for systems.
  • Ability to understand and present top-tier security conference papers.
  • Ability to conduct hands-on security research and write research papers.
  • Ability to understand and evaluate the application of various security techniques on 5G and NextG networks.

Grading

There are no exams or quizzes in this course! The course will be graded based on the research project, paper presentation, paper reviews, and class participation in the following proportions:

  • 50% Research project (including formal presentation and paper write-up)
  • 30% Paper presentations
  • 10% Paper reviews
  • 10% Class participation

Grades will be curved. A: ≥ 90, B: ≥ 80 and < 90, C: ≥ 70 and < 80, D: ≥ 60 and < 70, F: < 60.

Research Project

The big deliverable from this course is the course project. The expectation is a paper prepared for submission to a top security conference. A student can port their existing lab research for this course, provided that:

  1. It is related to network and system security;
  2. The student is the lead author;
  3. Within the timeframe of the course, substantial research can be done.

The instructor and student will have a discussion to decide ultimately.

Presentation and Participation

The student will present 3–4 papers throughout the course. In each presentation, students who are not presenters will try to ask questions and find a gap in the paper, whereas the presenters have to defend the paper. This is where the participation points will come from. If you do not participate, there are no points.

Paper Reviews

Each week, students will write a review (maximum two pages) for one of the papers assigned that week. A specific review format will be provided. Papers from the following conferences will be used:

  • IEEE S&P
  • CCS
  • NDSS
  • USENIX Security

GenAI Usage Policy

You are welcome to use ChatGPT, Claude, DeepSeek, Gemini, or any other tools. It is good to understand and know how to use these tools, and it’s not fair for the instructor to stop any student from using them. However, these tools should be used to understand concepts after going through the contents of the class and in no way as a reference or an alternative to the class, course book, or slides.

These tools hallucinate frequently, especially for technical networking content. They may sound confident but be incorrect. Similarly, they may be used to review papers, but such reviews are often poor and easily detectable. If you submit bad reviews, you will receive zero points.
