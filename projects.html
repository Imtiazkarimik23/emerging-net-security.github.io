Project

In this semester-long project, students will conduct research on network or systems security in teams of 1–2 people under the instructor's supervision. All teams and project topics must be approved by the instructor. The outcome of this project will be similar to a conference-style paper of at most 10 pages, in which you build an argument for studying the open research problems you advocate investigating—identifying why the problem is important and why it is solvable in the near future. The grade will be based on novelty, depth, correctness, rigor of execution, clarity of presentation, and effort.

Project Theme

“Thinking like an adversary” is critical to understanding (1) the current security and privacy posture of a system, (2) how existing security policies can be broken, and (3) how to enforce stronger and more robust security policies. Accordingly, each project should align with the theme of security analysis of an existing system.

The overarching goal is to design and implement a systematic security evaluation framework using program analysis, software testing, formal verification, and applied cryptography. Each project consists of two parts, each with multiple milestones.

Part 1 – Reproducing an Existing Attack

As the first step, you will reproduce a known attack on an existing system. Your task is to understand a known vulnerability and its corresponding attack and write an original exploit. You may not contact the paper authors for exploits or use pre-packaged attack tools. However, you may use open-source, general-purpose tools (e.g., gdb, Wireshark, packet sniffers) and scripts.

Some vulnerabilities may have been fixed in newer software or libraries (e.g., Linux, TCP, TLS), so you may need to target older versions.

According to the course ethics policy, you must not test attacks against systems owned by others. All attacks must be conducted against your own isolated device, machine, or virtual machine.

You will present a demo showing (1) the vulnerability, (2) how it can be exploited, and (3) the implications of the attack. You will also explain how you implemented the exploit, set up the attack environment, and the technical challenges encountered.

Part 2 – Project Extensions

Based on insights gained from Part 1, you may extend your project in one or more of the following directions:

  • Identify a new attack on the same system or protocol.
  • Design a systematic security analysis framework using formal verification, static or dynamic analysis, fuzzing, or other testing techniques.
  • Propose verified or high-assurance defenses.

Project Proposal

The proposal must not exceed two pages (including references). Use the ACM Overleaf template for all project-related submissions.

Proposal Components

  • What (Problem Statement): Project title, team member contact details, problem statement, goals, and the specific attack(s) to be reproduced.
  • Why (Motivation): Importance of the problem, relevant prior work, and why the vulnerability is critical and difficult to detect manually.
  • How (Design / Implementation): Resources required, plans for Part 2, related codebases, and evaluation criteria (e.g., effectiveness, efficiency).
  • Workload: Task distribution among team members and milestones for Part 1.

Final Report

You must submit a minimum 8-page (excluding references) conference- or workshop-style paper. Typical sections include Abstract, Introduction, Background, Design Overview, Design Details, Implementation, Evaluation, Discussion and Limitations, Related Work, and Conclusion and Future Work.

Abstract

An abstract should be relatively short: one paragraph, around 300 words. A wandering and off-topic abstract can confuse the reader. Consider the following template for writing an abstract (approximately one sentence per point):

  • Area
  • Problem
  • Solution Idea
  • Methodology
  • Results
  • Take-away

Introduction

The abstract and introduction are two important sections of your paper. An introduction is a longer version of the same outline as the abstract, often dedicating about a paragraph per point. This section motivates the research problem, clearly states the problem, explains why existing solutions are insufficient, presents the proposed ideas, provides high-level intuitions for why the approach should work, and summarizes the high-level results.

If you fail to capture the reviewer’s interest in the abstract and introduction, it is difficult to recover. These sections are also the most-read parts of a published paper.

Background

Provide only the background knowledge necessary to understand your paper. Do not include details that are never used or referenced. For example, in a paper on X.509 certificate validation, it is unnecessary to explain how public and private keys are generated or their key lengths.

Design Overview

Discuss the security assumptions, adversary capabilities and goals, and the trusted computing base. Summarize the design challenges of your proposed solution and provide high-level intuitions for addressing them. Finally, present a high-level overview of your system or solution.

You may use diagrams to illustrate the proposal. Without delving into detailed implementations, describe the functionality of each major component and the interactions among them using appropriate figures.

Design Details

Describe all components of your proposed system in detail and explain how critical corner cases are handled. These corner cases are important for enabling experts to fully understand your design and for allowing others to replicate or extend your work.

Always explain the intuition behind your design decisions—why you made certain choices is more important than how they were implemented. Keep the discussion at a conceptual level rather than focusing on low-level implementation details.

Implementation Details

Describe the software and hardware used, written, or modified in your implementation. Explain what each module does, how it relates to the system components, and how it supports the overall solution.

Evaluation

Evaluation Setup

Describe how experiments were set up, including evaluation metrics, datasets used (if any), and baseline results for comparison. Ensure that the metrics directly address the research questions.

Evaluation Results

Present results using tables and graphs. If different experiments use different setups, clearly describe the hardware, software, and datasets used. For informal security analysis, justify how the proposed solution prevents the attacks described in the threat model.

Related Work

Conduct a thorough investigation of related work. A good related work section is not a simple list of papers; instead, it tells a story of how prior research has evolved to address related problems. Clearly contrast your work with prior approaches rather than merely summarizing them.

Even if your research is ongoing, you should have enough understanding to compare your ideas with existing work. Pay attention to how related work sections are written in high-quality papers and identify what makes them effective or ineffective.

Conclusion and Future Work

Do not summarize the paper in this section. Instead, reflect on the broader implications of your work and discuss future research directions and possibilities that arise from your findings.
