Presentation Guidelines

Guidelines for Presentations

  • The length of the presentation is approximately 20 minutes.
  • All students must read the paper but are not required to go into exhaustive details (e.g., implementation details and related work).
  • Discuss the main components of the paper, including:
    • Motivation of the work
    • Problem statement
    • Required background knowledge
    • Threat model
    • Insights of the solution
    • Design overview
    • Key design details
    • Evaluation results
  • Provide your critiques (pros and cons) of the paper and summarize its limitations.
  • Identify open questions raised by the paper.
  • Lead the discussion by preparing several questions for the class. Example prompts include:
    • “We didn’t understand this part — can anybody explain?”
    • “Can you think of serious drawbacks or limitations of this work?”
    • “If this is an attack, what are some defenses? If it is a defense, what are some attacks?”
    • “What additional data should have been presented?”
  • On the title slide, identify the paper title, authors, and source, as well as your own names and the date. You may incorporate figures from the paper or other presentations, but you must cite the source on the slide. Do not simply reuse someone else’s presentation—use your own ideas and words.

Presentation Grading Rubric

  • [50 pts] Summarize the main points of the article.
  • [10 pts] Relate the article to previously presented articles and compare with related work.
  • [10 pts] Present the article in a clear, logical manner.
  • [10 pts] Critique the methodology used to reach the conclusions.
  • [10 pts] Justify opinions about the article.
  • [10 pts] Ask insightful questions for group discussion.
  • [10 pts] Facilitate a lively discussion of the article.
  • [10 pts] Moderate the discussion to ensure broad participation.
  • [20 pts] Act as an expert on the article content and answer clarifying questions.
  • [10 pts] Personalize the topic (e.g., sharing experiences or facilitating a class exercise).

Instructions for Participation

  • Read the papers being presented before class. In some cases, review cited references to understand the work.
  • Take notes while reading, as if reviewing the paper:
    • Is the paper written clearly and logically?
    • Are the findings original and important?
    • How does it compare with other work?
    • Are the conclusions solid and well justified?
    • What open problems or unanswered questions remain?
  • Come to class prepared to discuss the paper and answer questions.
